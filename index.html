Hi. We are Team 3.

And this is our project, Climate, Health and Socioeconomic Indicators of Disease Vulnerability in different areas in the Philippines using the Project CChain Dataset, turning open and linked data into practical insights that can help governments, communities, and organizations strengthen health systems, improve disaster preparedness, and better protect vulnerable populations from climate-related health risks.

Here's an overview of our project.

This project explores how climate, environment, and socioeconomic conditions influence the spread of climate-sensitive diseases in the Philippines. Using the Project CCHAIN dataset, which combines 20 years of health, climate, environmental, and socioeconomic data across 12 cities, we aim to uncover patterns that explain why some communities face higher risks than others. Our approach is to link disease data (like dengue or leptospirosis) with climate factors (temperature, rainfall, humidity), environmental hazards (floods, landslides), and social indicators (population density, wealth, access to healthcare). By doing this, we can identify the biggest drivers of disease vulnerability, highlight disparities between communities, and build predictive models that can give early warnings for outbreaks.

Motivation

We're motivated by one big question: why are some communities hit harder by climate-sensitive diseases than others? If we can answer that, we can help design smarter and fairer health programs.

Problem

In the Philippines, diseases like dengue, leptospirosis, and cholera often spike during floods, heavy rains, or sudden climate shifts. At the same time, not every community is affected the same way—some areas have better healthcare, safer environments, or more resources than others. The problem is that we don’t have a clear picture of how climate, environment, and social conditions all come together to affect disease risks.

Solution

Our project uses the Project CCHAIN dataset, which brings together health, climate, environmental, and socioeconomic data from different cities. By studying these factors side by side, we can see what really drives outbreaks and build models that help predict when and where diseases might spread. This way, local governments and communities can be better prepared and act early.
